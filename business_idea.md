**GOTA

Description of the digital business idea (500 Palabras)

Water utilities like Global Omnium already collect near real-time consumption data through smart meters and sensor networks, the same infrastructure used today to detect leaks before a customer notices them [1].  Yet this data mostly disappears into back-end operations, surfacing to the customer only once a month, as a number on an invoice. The business idea behind GOTA is to stop treating real-time water data as an operational byproduct, and instead use it to power a personalized, engaging, behavior-changing experience for every household.

GOTA is not new data infrastructure; it is an intelligence layer built on top of data the utility already owns. An AI engine continuously reads each household's consumption patterns and turns them into three things current apps don't offer:

1. Personalized tips and nudges. Instead of generic advice ("reduce your shower time"), GOTA generates recommendations grounded in each household's actual usage and readiness to change, drawing on behavioral frameworks shown to make water-saving recommendations more relevant and sustained than one-size-fits-all campaigns [2]. Tips evolve as behavior changes, so the app keeps giving relevant, timely guidance instead of static content nobody reads twice.

2. Awareness and incentives aligned with supply goals. Rather than only reporting consumption, GOTA actively encourages behaviors that match the utility's actual supply and sustainability targets — surfacing drought alerts, seasonal savings goals, or peak-demand reduction challenges, and rewarding progress with recognitions, small incentives, or friendly comparisons with similar households. This turns individual water-saving actions into something that visibly contributes to a shared, city-level goal, rather than an abstract bill-reduction exercise [3].

3. Customizable, adaptive dashboards. Not every customer wants the same view: some want a single urgent-only alert, others want granular, fixture-level detail, others respond better to gamified goals and streaks [4]. GOTA allows users to customize and optimize their dashboard accordingly, so the interface itself becomes personalized, not just the content inside it.

Underneath these features, self-service also becomes real-time and AI-assisted: billing questions, payment plans, and hardship support are resolved conversationally inside the app, instead of escalating to calls or branch visits — a gap that still shows up as a common source of customer frustration in the current experience [5].

The business model builds directly on infrastructure leading water suppliers already have, so there's no need for new hardware or new data pipelines; the investment is in AI, UX, and behavioral design. Value is captured through reduced customer-service costs, reduced non-revenue water losses, higher customer satisfaction and retention, and new B2G opportunities — such as offering municipalities anonymized, aggregated insights for drought and infrastructure planning [3].

What differentiates this from most utility apps in the sector, is the shift from a portal to an intelligent companion that proactively brings relevant, personalized action to the customer instead of "what to do next" [4]. The opportunity lies in further optimizing the data the utility already owns to think, adapt, and act on the customer's behalf.

<<Problem>> (950-1100 char)

- Leak detection still lags behind real usage: most billing-based systems only flag anomalies weeks after a meter read, long after a household leak has run up costs [3].
- Consumption dashboards show numbers, not meaning: customers get raw liters and euros, but rarely the personalized, behavior-based nudges that research on this same Valencia network shows drive lasting reductions in use [1].
- Digital self-service breaks down at the moments customers need it most: payment plan negotiations and hardship cases still require in-person visits, despite an app built to avoid exactly that trip [4].
- Engagement is shallow and short-lived: static usage reports and generic summaries fail to hold attention, and most utility apps stop at "what happened" instead of "what to do next" [5].
- One-size-fits-all design ignores that households want different things — urgent-only alerts for some, fixture-level detail for others — a mismatch most apps haven't solved [6].

<<Problem Tagline>> (One impactful sentence-120-145 char)

Water utility apps have digitized billing, but not water itself — customers still can't see, understand, or act on their real water use.

<<Solution>> (950-1100 char)

- GOTA adds an AI layer to smart-meter data utilities already collect, giving households personalized intelligence without new hardware.
- My Water turns readings into clear KPIs, 14-day forecasts, budget tracking, early anomaly signals, and ranked actions tailored to each home's patterns.
- Water Copilot explains changes, forecasts, possible leaks, and next steps conversationally while revealing the metrics behind each answer; Aqua, an optional mascot, keeps guidance approachable.
- My Community replaces district rankings with privacy-safe comparisons among similar homes, combining fair benchmarks with educational content and voluntary campaigns that link personal progress to shared water goals.
- Bill Explainer analyzes uploaded PDF or image bills beside the original, clarifies each charge, tracks historical totals, and supports a spending target. For utilities, this configurable B2B2C layer improves engagement, reduces avoidable support demand and water loss, and provides aggregated planning insight.

<<Solution Tagline>> (One impactful sentence-120-145 char)
GOTA turns existing meter and billing data into trusted forecasts, clear explanations and personalized actions that make saving water easier.

<<Market>> (950-1100 char)

- We are an independent platform company; the product is the engagement layer, licensed to water utilities as B2B2C SaaS. The utility pays per account for a layer that lowers its cost to serve and its water losses; above it we run a consumer layer, earning modestly from marketplace and premium tiers. Global Omnium is our first target and proving ground, not our owner.

- Revenue per account splits in two. A B2B licence near €1.50, justified by the cost a utility avoids rather than by an app price, and B2C monetisation near €1.50, held deliberately low. Blended, about €3 a year.

- TAM. The EU-27 has 202M households (Eurostat, 2024); as smart metering nears full coverage, at €3 each the pool approaches €600M a year.

- SAM, Spain. Its 19.4M households (INE, 2024), reachable as we sign their utilities, are worth about €58M a year.

- SOM. Winning Global Omnium, some 3M accounts with roughly a third active, is near €3M a year, and it turns on that one signature.

- Above the base sits the real upside: anonymised data for municipalities, adjacent utilities, and embedded leak insurance.

<<Market Tagline>> (One impactful sentence-120-145 char)

Metering made consumption visible, not intelligible; the layer that closes that gap earns from what the utility saves, a market near €600M. 

<<Competitors>> (950-1100 char)

- AMI and meter-data infrastructure vendors, such as Itron and Kamstrup, focus on network reliability and operational analytics, rarely converting data into engaging household experiences.
- WaterSmart, VertexOne, and Badger Meter's EyeOnWater deliver alerts, comparisons, and budgets, but remain static portals lacking adaptive, sustained engagement.
- Dropcountr converts AMI data into leak alerts and social norming, yet its model stays confined to self-service, without conversational guidance.
- SmartH2O piloted gamified water feedback in Valencia, but its research-era platform never evolved into a scalable, AI-personalized product.
- Oracle's Opower industrializes behavioral AI across utilities, but its logic targets energy load-shifting, treating water as a generic commodity.
- Consumer sensors like Hydrific's Droplet capture granular flow data, but require separate hardware, limiting scalable reach across utility customers.
<<Competitors Tagline>> (One impactful sentence-120-145 char)
The market is not primarily missing more water data. It is missing the intelligence and behavioural layer that converts existing data into sustained customer action.
